# Trust the Statement

*After a sentence delivers a complete, self-contained claim, the next sentence must either advance to new material or end the paragraph. Sentences that soften, reinforce, or explain a statement the reader already understands are violations.*

## The Principle

Some sentences are self-contained. They deliver a full proposition that a reader can process without additional support. Examples:

- "Learning Claude in 2026 is like buying Bitcoin in 2010."
- "Your mind is the asset."
- "The system runs itself."
- "Specificity is authority."

When a sentence of this type appears, the writer has two valid next moves: advance to a new point, or stop the paragraph. A third option — explaining what the statement means — is a violation. The reader does not need it. Adding it weakens the original statement.

This rule overlaps with the forward-motion rule but applies specifically to self-contained claims. Forward-motion says every sentence must add new information. Trust says: after a self-contained statement, even information-adding sentences may violate if they function as explanation rather than progression.

## Identifying Self-Contained Statements

A sentence is self-contained when it meets all three of these criteria:

1. **It makes a complete proposition.** The sentence asserts something that a reader can evaluate as true or false.

2. **It is under 15 words.** Compression is part of what makes the statement land.

3. **It does not reference a topic that requires definition.** A self-contained statement uses common-knowledge terms or terms the reader already has.

Examples that qualify:
- "The default experience is dumb." (9 words, complete proposition, terms understood)
- "Personality is the moat." (4 words, complete proposition, terms understood)
- "Claude writes like you, if you train it." (8 words, complete proposition, terms understood in context)

Examples that do not qualify (because they require follow-up to be understood):
- "The process is Bencivenga's Persuasion Equation applied in reverse." (requires definition)
- "There are seven steps to this." (announces a list; follow-up is required)

## The Test

After every self-contained statement, inspect the next sentence. Check whether it performs one of the following functions:

**Explanation.** The next sentence defines, clarifies, or re-paraphrases the statement. Example:

- Statement: "Learning Claude in 2026 is like buying Bitcoin in 2010."
- Violation: "The people who get in early will have a huge advantage over those who wait."

**Qualification.** The next sentence softens or limits the statement without adding substance. Example:

- Statement: "The system runs itself."
- Violation: "Well, it mostly runs itself, once you set it up properly."

**Reinforcement.** The next sentence agrees with the statement in different words. Example:

- Statement: "Personality is the moat."
- Violation: "Your unique voice is what sets you apart from competitors."

If any of these three functions applies, the next sentence is a violation.

## Valid Next Moves

After a self-contained statement, these functions are valid:

**Advance.** The next sentence introduces a new claim, example, or direction. Example:
- Statement: "Learning Claude in 2026 is like buying Bitcoin in 2010."
- Valid follow: "Most people reading this will do nothing with it."

**Terminate.** The paragraph ends. The self-contained statement is the final sentence.

**Specify.** The next sentence provides a concrete example, number, or named instance that makes the statement specific rather than explaining it. Example:
- Statement: "Specificity is authority."
- Valid follow: "When Halbert wrote the Coat of Arms letter, he addressed each reader by their family surname."

Specification differs from explanation. Explanation paraphrases the statement. Specification introduces new concrete material that demonstrates the statement.

## The Fix

When a self-contained statement is followed by a violation:

**First action: delete the violating sentence.** Most of the time, this completes the fix. The self-contained statement stands alone, and the paragraph either ends or moves to the next idea.

**Second action: if the paragraph needs to continue, replace the violating sentence with one that advances or specifies.** Do not rewrite the explanation into a better explanation. Replace the function entirely.

**Third action: if you cannot find an advancing or specifying sentence to follow, the paragraph should end here.** A paragraph that ends on a self-contained statement is structurally complete.

## The "One Breath" Rule

A paragraph may contain at most one self-contained statement. If a paragraph contains two, the weaker one should be cut or moved to a new paragraph. Stacking self-contained statements dilutes their effect — each one fights the others for emphasis.

## The Most Common Violation

The most common violation of this rule is the "one more for the road" pattern. The writer delivers a strong line and cannot resist adding one more sentence to make sure the reader got it. Example:

- Statement: "Your mind is the asset."
- Violation: "That's why protecting your attention matters so much."

The second sentence treats the reader as someone who did not understand the first. Trust that they did. Cut the follow-up.

## Detection Priority

This rule is second in priority after forward-motion. Run it on every paragraph after forward-motion has been applied, because the two rules catch related but distinct failures. Forward-motion catches all restatement. Trust catches specifically the explanation-after-a-strong-line pattern that forward-motion sometimes misses when the explanation technically adds a related point.
