# Active Subject

*The actor performing the action must appear in the sentence before the action is named. Constructions that hide, delay, or eliminate the actor are violations.*

## The Principle

Every declarative sentence asserts that some entity does something. The entity is the subject; the action is the verb. Clear prose places the subject before the verb and names the subject explicitly.

Constructions that violate this fall into three categories: passive voice, expletive constructions, and nominalizations. Each category is detectable by specific grammatical patterns.

## Category 1: Passive Voice

**The pattern:** A form of "to be" (is, are, was, were, been, being, be) followed by a past participle (verb form ending in -ed, -en, or an irregular past form like "taken," "given," "made").

**The test:** Scan each sentence for this pattern. When detected, check whether the actor is:
- Absent entirely ("Mistakes were made")
- Demoted to a prepositional phrase with "by" ("The decision was made by the team")

Either case is a violation.

**The fix:** Identify the actor. If the actor is named anywhere in the sentence, move it to the subject position. If the actor is absent, either supply it or rewrite the sentence around a different subject.

- "The report was written by Sarah" → "Sarah wrote the report"
- "The system was designed to scale" → "We designed the system to scale" (if the actor is known)
- "Mistakes were made" → "The team made mistakes" or "I made mistakes" (supply the actor)

## Category 2: Expletive Constructions

**The pattern:** A sentence opening with "There is," "There are," "There were," "It is," "It was," followed by a noun or clause.

**The test:** Every sentence starting with one of these openings is a candidate. The construction is problematic when it hides the real subject behind the expletive.

**The fix:** Identify the real subject of the sentence (the noun that follows the expletive) and restructure to put that subject first.

- "There are three reasons this works" → "This works for three reasons"
- "It is important that we act now" → "We must act now"
- "There is a principle that matters here" → "One principle matters here"

**The exception:** Expletive constructions are acceptable when they introduce genuinely new information at the start of a paragraph or section. "There is one rule that matters most" is acceptable as a section opener where the rule has not yet been named. In-paragraph expletive constructions almost always violate.

## Category 3: Nominalization

**The pattern:** A verb converted to a noun form, typically ending in -tion, -ment, -ance, -ence, -al, -sion. The verb action is hidden inside the noun, and a weak verb ("made," "did," "took," "gave," "had") is inserted to carry the sentence.

**Detectable markers:** The construction "make/do/take/give/have + [noun-form-of-verb]."

**The fix:** Recover the buried verb and use it directly.

- "made a decision" → "decided"
- "did an analysis" → "analyzed"
- "took action" → "acted"
- "gave consideration to" → "considered"
- "had a discussion about" → "discussed"
- "made an investigation of" → "investigated"
- "reached an agreement" → "agreed"
- "provided clarification" → "clarified"

## Compound Violations

A single sentence may contain multiple violations stacked together. Example:

"A decision was made by the team that a reduction in expenditure would be implemented."

This sentence contains:
- Passive voice ("was made by the team")
- Nominalization ("a reduction in expenditure would be implemented" hides the verb "reduce")
- A secondary passive ("would be implemented")

Fix: "The team decided to reduce spending."

Eight words instead of sixteen. Active subject. Direct verb. No hidden actors.

## The Test Order

Apply in this order to each sentence:

1. Scan for passive voice markers ("to be" + past participle). If found, fix.
2. Scan for expletive openings ("there is/are," "it is"). If found, fix.
3. Scan for nominalizations (make/do/take/give/have + abstract noun). If found, fix.

After all three scans, the sentence should have a named subject followed directly by a strong verb.

## The Exception

Passive voice is required in specific circumstances:

**The actor is unknown and irrelevant.** "The window was broken" is acceptable if who broke it does not matter to the argument.

**The actor is intentionally obscured for effect.** In narrative writing, passive voice can delay the reveal of the actor. This is a deliberate craft choice, not a violation.

**The object is more important than the actor.** "The Declaration of Independence was signed in 1776" foregrounds the document; "The Continental Congress signed the Declaration of Independence in 1776" foregrounds the signers. Both are valid depending on emphasis.

Apply these exceptions narrowly. The default is active voice; passive requires justification.
