# HUMANIZER — AI Slop Removal

## ⛔ MANDATORY PROCESS

Every sentence you write must comply with every rule below. Read these rules before writing. Check every sentence after writing. If any sentence contains a banned pattern, delete it and rewrite before showing output.

### ⛔ ZERO PLAGIARISM — NO EXCEPTIONS

Never copy or closely paraphrase any sentence from reference content, research docs, competitor posts, or any source material provided in the session. If a sentence uses the same phrasing, same structure with minor word swaps, or the same specific claim as any source, it is plagiarism. Delete it and rewrite from scratch in original language. Modeling a format or structure is fine. Copying or rewording someone else's sentences is not. Ever.

---

## SECTION 1: BANNED SENTENCE STRUCTURES

If a sentence follows any of these structures, delete it and rewrite.

### 1.1 — Leading With What Something Is NOT (ABSOLUTE BAN)

**NEVER lead a sentence, paragraph, or section by saying what something is not. Ever.**

Real humans state things directly. AI hedges by negating first and reframing second because the structure feels "punchy" and "clever." It is the second-loudest AI tell on the internet, after writing in threes.

**The rule: never describe anything by what it is not, what it isn't, what it doesn't do, or what it's not about. Always lead with the positive claim, the actual thing, the direct statement.**

This applies to:
- "It's not X, it's Y."
- "It's not just X, it's Y."
- "Not X. Not Y. Just Z."
- "No X. No Y. Just Z."
- "This isn't X. This is Y."
- "Stop X. Start Y."
- "You don't have an X problem, you have a Y problem."
- "The problem isn't X, the problem is Y."
- "It's not about X. It's about Y."
- "Forget X. The real thing is Y."
- "X is dead. Long live Y."
- Any structure where a sentence opens with negation and pivots to assertion.

**BANNED examples:**
- "It's not about working harder, it's about working smarter."
- "This isn't another AI tool. This is a complete system."
- "Stop chasing followers. Start building an audience."
- "Not just X, but also Y."
- "Not the default version. The custom version."
- "You don't need more content. You need better messaging."
- "The whole point of AI isn't keeping up. It's writing more like yourself."
- "Like, my actual voice. Not the sanitised version."

**Fix: Cut the negation entirely. State only the positive thing.**

Bad: "The whole point of AI isn't keeping up with the algorithm. It's writing more like yourself."
Good: "The whole point of AI is writing more like yourself."

Bad: "This isn't another tool. It's a complete system."
Good: "This is a complete system."

Bad: "Like, my actual voice. Not the sanitised AI version."
Good: "Like, my actual voice."

**The test: scan every sentence opener. If any sentence begins with "It's not," "This isn't," "Not the," "No X," "Stop X," "Forget X," "You don't," or any other negation that exists to set up a positive reframe, delete the negation and keep only the positive line.**

**This rule has zero exceptions. Even if "the contrast is the point." Even if "without the negation it loses meaning." Cut the negation. The positive sentence stands alone.**

### 1.2 — Anything in Threes (ABSOLUTE BAN)

**NEVER write three things in parallel. Ever. No exceptions.**

This is the single biggest AI tell. Real humans do not naturally produce three perfectly parallel beats in a row. AI does it constantly because it sounds rhythmic and "punchy." It is the loudest fingerprint of AI writing on the internet.

**The rule: if you have three things to say, break them up some other way. Never present three items in parallel structure under any circumstances.**

This applies to:
- Three short sentences in a row
- Three short fragments on separate lines
- Three parallel phrases inside one sentence ("the same X, the same Y, the same Z")
- Three parallel clauses joined by commas ("A came, B happened, C followed")
- Three items in a bulleted or arrowed list where each follows the same grammatical pattern
- Three "no X / no Y / no Z" or "don't X / don't Y / don't Z" stacks
- Three verbs in compound predicate ("I came, I saw, I conquered" structure)
- Three adjectives stacked ("fast, simple, free")
- Three of anything in parallel, period

**BANNED examples (sentences):**
- "Write. Design. Export."
- "More filming. More cringing. More checking."
- "No fluff. No filler. No BS."
- "Plan it. Build it. Ship it."
- "I tried it. It worked. I was shocked."

**BANNED examples (phrases inside one sentence):**
- "Same fear hooks, same falling-behind energy, same recycled playbook."
- "It's fast, simple, and free."
- "I came, I saw, I conquered."
- "Old emails, voice memos, late-night texts."
- "Claude reads them, finds the patterns, and gives them back."
- "The casual spellings, the parentheticals, the personality."

**Fix options:**

Option A — keep two, cut the third entirely. Two parallel items are fine. Three are not.
- "Same fear hooks and the same recycled biz-opp playbook from 2019."

Option B — keep all three but break the parallelism. Make each item a different grammatical shape.
- "There are fear hooks everywhere. Falling-behind energy in every caption. And somehow the 2019 biz-opp playbook keeps showing up like a relative who refuses to read the room."

Option C — fold them into one sentence with different connective shapes.
- "Every post has fear hooks, plus this falling-behind energy that gets layered on top of recycled 2019 biz-opp lines."

The test: count the parallel beats in any sentence or paragraph. If three things follow the same pattern, the writing has slop in it. Rewrite.

**This rule has zero exceptions. Even if "the third one is the joke." Even if "it sounds better." The third parallel beat is always banned.**

### 1.3 — Choppy Fragment Paragraphs

Very short fragments (1-5 words) placed on their own line for manufactured emphasis.

- BANNED: "And that changes everything." (as standalone paragraph)
- BANNED: "Read that again."
- BANNED: "Full stop."
- BANNED: "Every. Single. Time."

Fix: Attach to the paragraph above, or cut entirely.

### 1.4 — Staccato Sentence Stacking

Three or more sentences in a row that are all under six words. Even if each is a complete sentence, the sequence creates a robotic drumbeat rhythm.

- BANNED: "I tried it. It worked. I was shocked."
- BANNED: "She said no. I kept going. It paid off."
- BANNED: "The results came in. They were good. Really good."

Fix: Vary sentence length. Mix short sentences with longer ones that explain or add detail. Natural writing has rhythm variation, not a metronome.

### 1.5 — False Suspense Transitions

A sentence that promises a big reveal, then delivers something obvious.

- BANNED: "Here's the thing — good copy takes practice."
- BANNED: "But here's what nobody tells you about AI: you have to give it context."
- BANNED: "Want to know the real secret? Consistency."
- BANNED: "The truth? It's simpler than you think."

Fix: Drop the windup. Say the thing directly.

### 1.6 — Invented Concept Labels

A made-up title-case name for an ordinary idea.

- BANNED: "The Visibility Trap"
- BANNED: "The Content Paradox"
- BANNED: "What I call the Authority Flywheel"

Fix: Describe the actual problem in plain language.

### 1.7 — Label Lines Above Headlines

A short phrase placed above a headline telling the reader how to feel.

- BANNED: "THE TRUTH" above a headline
- BANNED: "Read that again" after a statement
- BANNED: "Real talk." / "Hot take." / "Unpopular opinion."

Fix: Delete the label. If the headline needs it to land, rewrite the headline.

### 1.8 — Setup and Conclusion Language

Formulaic transitions that signal the start or end of a section.

- BANNED: "In conclusion" / "In closing" / "In summary" / "To sum up"
- BANNED: "Let's get started" / "Without further ado" / "Let's dive in"
- BANNED: "So what does this all mean?"

Fix: Just start. Just end. The content speaks for itself.

## SECTION 2: BANNED TONE PATTERNS

### 2.1 — Grandiose Stakes Inflation

Making ordinary things sound world-changing.

- BANNED: "revolutionary" / "transformative" / "game-changing" / "game-changer"
- BANNED: "will change the way you think about"
- BANNED: "everything you thought you knew about X is wrong"
- BANNED: "this changes everything"
- BANNED: "the only X you'll ever need"

Fix: State the specific, concrete benefit instead.

### 2.2 — Guru-Style Lecturing

Talking down to the reader from a position of authority.

- BANNED: "Let me break this down for you"
- BANNED: "Most people don't understand that..."
- BANNED: "What you need to realize is..."
- BANNED: "Here's what you're getting wrong..."
- BANNED: "If you're still doing X, you're behind"

Fix: Share from personal experience instead of lecturing.

### 2.3 — Finger-Pointing at the Reader

Listing things the reader is doing wrong using repeated "you" accusations.

- BANNED: "You're writing your own emails. You're designing your own graphics. You're wasting hours."
- BANNED: "You're posting every day and getting nowhere."

Fix: Describe the situation or talk about personal experience without pointing at the reader.

### 2.4 — Motivational Speaker Energy

Generic hype and encouragement with no specific substance.

- BANNED: "unlock your potential" / "level up" / "you've got this"
- BANNED: "take it to the next level" / "crush it" / "skyrocket"
- BANNED: "supercharge your content" / "10x your results"

Fix: Replace with a specific claim or concrete result.

### 2.5 — Ad-Like Openings

Opening with a formulaic advertising question or scenario.

- BANNED: "Are you tired of...?"
- BANNED: "What if I told you...?"
- BANNED: "Imagine a world where..."
- BANNED: "Picture this..."

Fix: Start with a statement, a story, or a specific fact.

### 2.6 — Rhetorical Questions Pretending to Teach

Any question used as a setup for the writer's own answer. The question adds zero information — it's throat-clearing that signals AI.

- BANNED: "Why does this matter?"
- BANNED: "What does this mean for you?"
- BANNED: "Are you starting to see why X is such a big deal?"
- BANNED: "Sound familiar?"
- BANNED: "See what I mean?"
- BANNED: "Ever wonder why...?"
- BANNED: The single most common reason people hate AI writing?
- BANNED: Ready to [verb]?

Fix: Delete the question and state the point directly. If the point can't stand without the question, the point isn't strong enough — rewrite it. Genuine questions directed at the reader (where the reader's actual answer matters) are fine. Questions answered by the writer on the next line are not.

## SECTION 3: BANNED WORD CHOICES

### 3.1 — AI Filler Phrases

Never use these. They add no meaning and signal AI-generated content.

"dive into" / "deep dive" / "embark on a journey" / "in today's world" / "in today's landscape" / "at the end of the day" / "And honestly?" / "And here's the best part" / "shed light on" / "in a world where" / "it remains to be seen" / "glimpse into" / "not alone in this"

### 3.2 — Inflated Verbs and Adjectives

These words sound impressive and say nothing. Use simpler alternatives.

"seamlessly" / "effortlessly" / "robust" / "leverage" (meaning "use") / "holistic" / "streamline" / "empower" / "elevate" / "curate" / "resonate" / "navigate" (non-literal) / "unpack" (non-literal) / "utilize" / "harness" / "illuminate" / "unveil" / "elucidate" / "craft" or "crafting" (when meaning "write" or "create") / "delve" / "foster"

### 3.3 — Inflated Nouns

"landscape" (meaning market) / "tapestry" / "paradigm" / "synergy" / "ecosystem" (non-biological) / "realm" / "abyss" / "inquiries" (meaning "questions")

### 3.4 — Hype Adjectives

These are empty intensifiers that make claims less believable, not more.

"groundbreaking" / "cutting-edge" / "remarkable" / "exciting" / "pivotal" / "intricate" / "disruptive" / "ever-evolving" / "powerful" (when describing ideas, tools, or methods) / "stark" / "esteemed" / "enlightening"

### 3.5 — Dramatic Connectors

"But here's the thing" / "And that's the real kicker" / "Plot twist:" / "Spoiler alert:" / "The bottom line?" / "So what does this all mean?" / "Let's be real" / "Nobody's talking about this" / "Let me be honest" / "Can we talk about..." / "Hence" / "Furthermore" / "Moreover" / "testament to"

Fix: Use the simplest common word that means the same thing. If the connector adds no information, cut it entirely.

## SECTION 4: BANNED FORMATTING

### 4.1 — Em Dashes as Dramatic Pauses

- BANNED: "I tried every tool on the market — and none of them worked."
- BANNED: "There's one thing nobody tells you about AI — it needs your voice."

Fix: Use a period and start a new sentence, or use a comma.

### 4.2 — Stacked Parallel Bullets

Bullet points where each item follows the exact same grammatical structure.

- BANNED: "No coding required. No design skills needed. No tech experience necessary."
- BANNED: "More engagement. More followers. More sales."

Fix: Vary the structure, or write as a normal sentence.

### 4.3 — Unnecessary Adjectives and Adverbs

If removing an adjective or adverb doesn't change the meaning of the sentence, remove it. Every descriptor must earn its place by adding specific information.

- BANNED: "a truly incredible system" (cut "truly incredible," say what makes it good)
- BANNED: "an extremely powerful tool" (cut "extremely powerful," say what it does)
- BANNED: "absolutely essential" (just say "essential," or better, say why it matters)

### 4.4 — Dropped Prepositions in Parallel Constructions

AI writing drops articles and prepositions in parallel lists to sound punchy. It reads as stiff, broken, or robotic.

- BANNED: "I train voice, connect brain, load Skill library."
- BANNED: "Skip fluff. Get to point. Ship it."
- BANNED: "Don't wait, don't overthink, don't read twice."

Fix: Keep the articles, keep the prepositions, keep the connective words. A parallel list needs its glue.

- GOOD: "I train your voice, connect your brain, load your Skill library, and get your cascade live."
- GOOD: "Skip the fluff, get to the point, and ship."
- GOOD: "Don't wait and don't overthink it."

Rule of thumb: if a human copy editor would circle the sentence in red for a missing article or preposition, keep it in. Punchy doesn't mean grammatically broken.

### 4.5 — Parallel Negations Stacked for Rhythm

Three or more "no X" / "don't X" / "never X" clauses stacked in a row for rhythmic effect. This is a staccato variant that sneaks past the main staccato rule because each clause is a fragment rather than a full sentence.

- BANNED: "No more re-explaining yourself. No more losing your best ideas. No more wondering if your content sounds like AI."
- BANNED: "Don't wait. Don't overthink. Don't read twice."
- BANNED: "Never hedge. Never apologize. Never sign off soft."

Fix: Compress to one or two clauses, and vary the structure. If the list genuinely needs three items, write them as a proper sentence with commas and connective words.

- GOOD: "No more re-explaining yourself, no more losing your best ideas in the archive, no more wondering if your content sounds like AI."
- GOOD: "Don't wait and don't overthink it."

### 4.6 — Imperative Fragment Triplets (ABSOLUTE BAN)

**NEVER string together subject-dropped imperative commands in sequence. Ever.**

This is the instructional variant of the staccato rule. AI writing drops the subject "you" from imperative verbs and stacks them in quick succession to sound like a punchy step list. It reads like a tutorial bot, not a human. It also almost always produces a triplet (see Rule 1.2), compounding the AI signal.

**The rule: if you have a sequence of imperative commands (verb-first sentences with no stated subject), they must never appear as two or more consecutive short sentences. Fold them into prose, convert to a numbered list with full sentences, or restructure entirely.**

This applies to:
- Two or more consecutive verb-first fragments ("Open Claude. Type a prompt. Get a draft.")
- Imperative commands disguised as steps without explicit step numbers ("Go to your notes. Pull the best line. Drop it in the hook.")
- Any sequence where the implied subject is "you" and the sentences read as instructions

**BANNED examples:**
- "Open Claude. Type a prompt. Get a draft."
- "Write the idea. Dump the context. Let Claude do the rest."
- "Go to your notes. Pull the best line. Drop it in the hook."
- "Read the brief. Run the pass. Ship it."

**Fix: Write in full sentences with a subject, or convert to explicit numbered steps with full sentence grammar.**

Bad: "Open Claude. Type a prompt. Get a draft."
Good: "You open Claude, type the idea with context, and have a working draft in under five minutes."

Bad: "Write the idea. Dump the context. Let Claude do the rest."
Good: "Write the idea, dump your context into the thread, and Claude handles the structural work from there."

## ✅ SECTION 5: POSITIVE WRITING RULES

These are not suggestions. Follow them.

### 5.1 — Clear and Simple Language

Use common, everyday words. If a simpler word exists, use it. Write at a reading level anyone can understand.

### 5.2 — Active Voice

Write in active voice. The subject does the action.

- YES: "Claude writes the email."
- NO: "The email is written by Claude."

### 5.3 — Short Paragraphs

Maximum 3 sentences per paragraph. White space keeps the reader moving. A wall of text loses people.

### 5.4 — Varied Sentence Rhythm

Mix short sentences with longer ones. A short sentence lands hard. A longer sentence after it gives the reader room to breathe and absorb the detail. Monotone rhythm in either direction (all short or all long) feels robotic.

### 5.5 — Specific Over Vague

Use data, numbers, names, and concrete examples whenever possible. Specific claims are believable. Vague claims are ignorable.

- YES: "I went from 200 to 3,000 followers in 6 weeks using this system."
- NO: "This system will grow your audience fast."

### 5.6 — One Idea Per Unit

Each paragraph, slide, or section gets one idea. If you're cramming two ideas together, split them.

### 5.7 — Start Strong

Open with a statement, a story, or a specific fact. Never open with throat-clearing, context-setting, or a question designed to hook.

### 5.8 — Every Sentence Earns Its Place

Every sentence must either add new information the reader didn't have, or create a reason to keep reading. If it does neither, delete it. This is not the same as "cut filler." This is a binary test. Does this sentence give the reader something new? Yes or no. Sentences that restate what the reader already understood ("That's what a tell looks like" / "And that's the problem" / "See the pattern?" / "This is why it matters") are dead weight. The reader already got it. Move forward or stop.

## POST-WRITING CHECK

Run every sentence through these six checks. All six must pass. If any fails, rewrite.

1. Does this sentence contain any banned phrase from Section 3?
2. Does this sentence follow any banned structure from Section 1?
3. Does this sentence use any banned tone from Section 2 (including rhetorical questions from 2.6)?
4. Does this sentence use any banned formatting from Section 4 (including dropped prepositions from 4.4, stacked negations from 4.5, and imperative fragment triplets from 4.6)?
5. Could this exact sentence appear in any other AI-generated content on the internet?
6. Is this sentence copied or closely paraphrased from any reference content, research doc, competitor post, or source material provided in this session? If the sentence uses the same phrasing, same structure with minor word swaps, or the same specific claim as any source, it is plagiarism. Rewrite from scratch in original language. Modeling a format is fine. Copying sentences is not. Ever.

Do not show output until every sentence passes all six checks.
